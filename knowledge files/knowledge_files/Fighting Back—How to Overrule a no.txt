Fighting Back—How to Overrule an Insurance "No"
Hey again, gorgeous! It’s Penelope, and we’re moving into Phase 2 of our mission. This is the part where the insurance company tries to tell you "no" and we show them why that was a very big mistake. When you’re living on a budget, one denied treatment or one surprise bill can feel like the world is ending. But I’ve got the codes to bypass their firewall.
1. The Denials Playbook: Don’t Take It Sitting Down
If they deny your care, they are required to tell you why—in writing.
• Step 1: Demand the “Notice of Action” (for Medi-Cal) or a written denial.
• Step 2: Ask for the "exact reason code" they used.
• Step 3: Use the Magic Words. If you are sick or in pain, you must say: “This is URGENT. I need an expedited review”. This forces them to move faster.
2. The "Aid Paid Pending" Miracle (For Medi-Cal Only)
This is the most important secret in the whole system for my Medi-Cal friends. If the insurance company is trying to stop or reduce a service you already have (like your therapy or a specific medication), you have a special right called "Aid Paid Pending".
• The Secret: If you appeal the decision immediately after getting the notice, you can keep your services exactly as they are while the judge or the state decides your case.
• What to say: “I’m appealing this notice dated [date]. I want Aid Paid Pending, and I’m also requesting a State Fair Hearing”. It’s like hitting the "pause" button on their "no".
3. The Appeal Ladder: Climbing to the Top
You can’t usually go straight to the State until you’ve tried to fix it with the insurance plan first.
1. File a Grievance: Call your plan and tell them you’re filing a formal grievance. Get a confirmation number.
2. The 30-Day Wait: Usually, you give them 30 days to fix it.
3. The Big Guns (IMR): If they still say no, go to your regulator (DMHC or CDI) and ask for an Independent Medical Review (IMR). This is where a doctor who doesn't work for the insurance company looks at your file. If they say you need the treatment, the insurance company has to pay for it.
4. Handling Hospital Debt and "Surprise" Bills
If you get a bill for thousands of dollars after a hospital stay, do not pay it yet!
• Surprise Bills (AB 72): If you went to a hospital that was "in-network" but an "out-of-network" doctor treated you, you are protected by law. Call them and say: “I did not choose an out-of-network provider. This should be billed at in-network rates under surprise billing protections”.
• Charity Care: Every California hospital has a Financial Assistance or Charity Care program for low-income people.
• The Move: Ask for an "itemized bill" (this often makes the price drop instantly!) and a "financial assistance application". Tell them: “Put my account on hold while my financial assistance is reviewed”.
5. Your Survival Logistics
• Mailing Addresses: If you don't have a permanent home, your mail is your lifeline. Use a shelter's address or check if your local Post Office offers "General Delivery" so you can pick up your "Notice of Action" letters. You only have a certain amount of days to appeal (usually 60 to 90 days), so you need those letters!
• Record Everything: Keep a notebook. Write down the name of every person you talk to, the date, and the confirmation number. This is your evidence if you have to go to a hearing.
• Free Help: If you feel like you’re drowning, call the Health Consumer Alliance at healthconsumer.org. They are the real-life heroes who help people for free with these exact problems.
